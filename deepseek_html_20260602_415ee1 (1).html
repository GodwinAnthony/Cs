📘 Introduction

Railway automation systems are at the heart of modern transportation, demanding precise speed regulation for safety, energy efficiency, and passenger comfort. Traditional train control often suffers from overshoot, oscillations, and instability due to nonlinear dynamics. Control systems play a crucial role in transportation, and the PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) controller remains the industry standard due to its simplicity and effectiveness. MATLAB & Simulink provide a powerful environment for model-based design, enabling engineers to simulate, tune, and validate controllers before deployment. This project focuses on a toy train system representing a two-degree-of-freedom mass-spring-damper configuration. The PID controller is implemented to track a step-up/step-down velocity command, suppressing disturbances and ensuring smooth speed transitions. Using Simulink, we model the electromechanical dynamics and analyze time-domain specifications. Model-based system design reduces prototyping costs and accelerates development cycles for railway automation, autonomous vehicles, and robotic systems. The visual feedback loop ensures adaptive correction, where the controller minimizes error dynamically. This portfolio demonstrates the complete workflow from mathematical derivation to simulation analysis, highlighting the effectiveness of PID in regulating a toy train's speed despite friction and coupling forces.

⚠️ Problem Statement

Traditional train speed control systems face substantial challenges: overshoot beyond desired velocity, prolonged oscillations, unstable speed tracking, passenger discomfort from jerk, and poor energy efficiency due to unnecessary acceleration surges. Without an adaptive feedback mechanism, open-loop control cannot compensate for friction, gradient changes, or mass variations. This project addresses the need for a robust PID-based closed-loop controller that minimizes rise time, eliminates steady-state error, and ensures comfort. The toy train system exhibits mass-spring coupling, which introduces oscillatory modes. The key is to design a controller that damps oscillations while following step commands. Our PID solution resolves instability and delivers smooth tracking with minimal overshoot (5.2%) and settling time (15 sec).

The PID-controlled toy train exhibits a stable closed-loop response. Following the step command at 10 seconds, the velocity rises rapidly and reaches a peak value of approximately 0.812 m/s. Overshoot of 5.2% is observed before settling around 0.772 m/s. At 150 seconds, the reference returns to zero, velocity decreases with minor undershoot (-0.038 m/s) and settles at zero, confirming controller effectiveness.
